# Ironlace Studio — LinkedIn

## Founder launch post

I opened the garage door this week.

Ironlace Studio is live — a one-person CNC shop in a 400-square-foot garage, cutting walnut, steel, aluminum, and brass into editioned, shop-made objects. Signage, inlays, wall pieces, custom hardware for the obsessive remodeler.

For two years I sold out of DMs and direct emails. It worked until it didn't. When I went full-time, the admin time ate the cutting time. So I built the store I wished existed:

→ A real catalog, with shop-floor photography
→ Stripe checkout with live shipping rates on crated pieces
→ A commission intake that turns a vector file into a quote in a day
→ A customer dashboard so people see their piece move from cut to crate

I built this on AWS Amplify — auth, data, file storage for customer vectors, all in one stack. Stripe for payments, webhooks for order state. For a shop of one, the leverage of modern infra is hard to overstate.

The site is at ironlacestudio.com. Trade pricing available for designers and architects.

If you know someone furnishing a remodel, specifying a storefront sign, or looking for a piece that wasn't stamped out by the thousand — I'd be grateful for the intro.

Precision cut. Artfully made.

## Process post (evergreen)

A thing I've learned running a one-person shop:

Clients don't want "premium." They want honest.

Show them the grain. Show them the toolpath. Show them the cut that didn't go right and what you did about it. That's the content that converts — not the hero shot.

Every product page on our new site has a shop-floor photo alongside the finished piece. Sometimes the finished piece is on a cardboard box. Sometimes the tape measure is still in frame. It outperforms studio photography by a wide margin.

The takeaway for anyone selling a handmade good: your product is the process. Don't hide it behind a softbox.
