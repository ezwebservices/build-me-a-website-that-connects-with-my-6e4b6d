# Ironlace Studio — Email Sequence

A six-email welcome sequence for the newsletter / first-purchase nurture. Voice: crafted, sensory, short sentences. No hype. Sent from the shop, not from a brand.

---

## Email 1 — Welcome (sent immediately)

**Subject:** Welcome to the garage.
**Preheader:** A short note about what you just signed up for.

Hey —

Thanks for leaving your email on the shop door.

Here's what you're in for: one note a week, usually Friday, usually short. What's on the bench, what's shipping, what I botched and salvaged. No sales calendar. No "last chance" anything.

If a piece goes up for sale, you'll hear about it here a day before it's public. That's the only perk, and it's a real one — most of the editioned work sells in a weekend.

The shop is at ironlacestudio.com if you want to wander.

— Signed, the guy with sawdust on his jeans.

## Email 2 — The story (day 3)

**Subject:** Why the shop is called Ironlace.
**Preheader:** It's not as romantic as it sounds.

The name came from a failure.

I was cutting a decorative steel panel for a friend's front porch. Fine pattern, 14-gauge, way too ambitious for the machine I had at the time. Halfway through the toolpath, a tab let go, the piece shifted, and what came off the bed was a tangled, ragged lattice of half-cut steel.

My friend looked at it and said, "That's beautiful. It looks like iron lace."

He took it home. Hung it over his fireplace. Named the shop for me, basically.

The lesson stuck: the mistakes are part of the material. Show them, keep them, sometimes sell them. You'll see that philosophy in everything in the store.

ironlacestudio.com

## Email 3 — Materials (day 7)

**Subject:** What we cut, and why.
**Preheader:** Walnut, steel, aluminum, brass. Four materials, on purpose.

A small catalog is a feature, not a bug.

Four materials. Four reasons.

**Black walnut** — because it darkens in sunlight over a year, and a piece that changes is a piece you keep noticing.

**Hot-rolled steel** — because blackened and waxed, it reads as heirloom, not industrial. The mill scale stays on where it wants to.

**6061 aluminum** — because brushed aluminum is underrated, and it's the only metal that looks equally at home in a modern kitchen and a workshop.

**Brass** — for pins, inlays, and the one accent that makes the rest of the piece sing.

No plywood. No MDF. No painted finishes that hide the grain. If we can't show the material honestly, we don't cut it.

See the current stock: ironlacestudio.com

## Email 4 — Commissions (day 12)

**Subject:** Send me a vector.
**Preheader:** How custom work actually happens in the shop.

Here's how a commission goes.

1. You upload a vector — SVG, DXF, AI, PDF with paths. If you have a sketch on a napkin, that's fine too. I'll vectorize it.
2. Pick a material. Pick a finish. Tell me the size.
3. I quote it within a business day. If it's weird, I'll call.
4. I cut it within a week or two of deposit.
5. You get a shop-floor photo before it leaves the bench. Approve it, and it ships crated, tracked, insured.

Roughly 60% of shop revenue is custom work. Signage for a new restaurant. A range hood detail for a kitchen remodel. A set of numbered panels for a designer's showroom.

Intake form: ironlacestudio.com/commission

## Email 5 — Process film (day 18)

**Subject:** Forty minutes of spindle, thirty seconds of film.
**Preheader:** A short video from this week's bench.

Short one today.

I filmed the cut on a walnut-and-brass piece this week, then sped it up. Forty minutes of spindle time, compressed to thirty seconds. You can watch it here: ironlacestudio.com/journal

The thing I love about watching these back is how much of the work isn't cutting. It's setup. Clamping. Probing. Zeroing. Walking around the machine three times to make sure nothing's going to launch.

The cut itself is the easy part. Everything else is the craft.

## Email 6 — Invitation (day 25)

**Subject:** If you've been thinking about a piece.
**Preheader:** Here's a small thing I can offer.

No urgency, no discount theater.

If a piece in the shop has been sitting in your head for a couple weeks, reply to this email and tell me about it. I'll send you a shop-floor photo of the current inventory of that piece — what the grain actually looks like, which number in the edition is left, where the brass pin landed.

If it's a custom idea, tell me that too. I'll draft a rough quote and a timeline, no obligation.

The shop is small enough that every sale is a conversation. I'd rather have that conversation than send you a coupon.

ironlacestudio.com

— Signed, the guy with sawdust on his jeans.
